⚛️ Ions & Electrons: Interactive Summary

Part 1: Ionisation Energy (IE)

What is First Ionisation Energy (IE₁)?

It is the energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.

Why do successive IEs always increase?

Because each electron is being removed from an ion with an increasingly positive charge. This stronger electrostatic attraction makes it harder to remove the next electron.

What is the trend in IE across a period and why?

It generally increases. This is because the nuclear charge increases, while shielding remains relatively constant, pulling the outer electrons more strongly.

What are the two "dips" in the trend across a period?
  • Group 2 to 3: A slight decrease occurs because the electron is removed from a higher-energy p-subshell.
  • Group 5 to 6: A slight decrease occurs due to spin-pair repulsion, making it easier to remove one of the paired electrons.
What is the trend in IE down a group and why?

It decreases. This is because the effect of increased electron shielding and a larger atomic radius outweighs the increase in nuclear charge, so the outer electron is held less tightly.

Part 2: Quantum Shells & Orbitals

What is an Atomic Orbital?

It is a region within a subshell with a high probability of finding an electron. Each orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons.

What are the shapes and capacities of s and p orbitals?
  • s-orbital: Spherical shape. Holds a maximum of 2 electrons.
  • p-orbitals: Dumbbell shape. Three orbitals per subshell, holding a maximum of 6 electrons.

Part 3: Electronic Configurations

What are the three main rules for filling orbitals?
  1. Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest energy orbitals first (1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d...).
  2. Hund's Rule: Place electrons singly in orbitals before pairing them up.
  3. Pauli Exclusion Principle: Two electrons in the same orbital must have opposite spins.
How are ion configurations determined?
  • Anions (negative): Add electrons to the next available orbital.
  • Cations (positive): Remove electrons from the orbital with the highest principal quantum number first. For transition metals, this means removing from the 4s orbital before the 3d.
What are the exceptions for Cr and Cu?

They promote a 4s electron to the 3d subshell to achieve a more stable half-filled or full d-subshell.

  • Cr: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹
  • Cu: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹
